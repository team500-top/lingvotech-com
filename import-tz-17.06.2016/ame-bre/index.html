Introduction
American English has grown steadily in international significance since World War II, parallel to the growth of U.S. political, economic, technological and cultural influence worldwide. American English is currently the dominant influence on "world English" (cf. British English) largely due to the following:
    \n
  1. Population: U.S. vs U.K. (SAE/SBE ca 70% vs 17% of all native English; Dibul #68)
    2. \n
  2. Wealth of the U.S. economy vs. the U.K., & influences
    3. \n
  3. Magnitude of higher education in America vs the U.K.
    4. \n
  4. Magnitude of the publishing industry in America
    5. \n
  5. Magnitude of global mass media and media technology influence
    6. \n
  6. Appeal of American popular culture on language and habits
    7. \n
  7. International political and economic position of the U.S. (cf. Kennedy)
    8. \n
American and British English are both variants of World English. As such, they are more similar than different, especially with "educated" or "scientific" English. Most divergence can be ascribed to differing national histories and cultural development (cf. Are Americans Ruining English? [PBS]), and the way in which the two national variants have changed correspondingly. The following general categories of difference between standard American English (SAE) and standard British English (SBE) each have their own sociolectic value: \n

      • \n
    • 'Honorifics': Mr. or Mrs. or Dr. Smith (U.S.) vs Mr or Mrs or Dr Smith (GB), etc.
      • \n
    \nGrammar
      \n
    1. (U.S.) Finnair has a flight to London today.
      (G.B.) Finnair have a flight to London today. (large collective nouns)
      2. \n
    2. (U.S.) England has (...) played well today, even if it lost.
      (G.B.) England have played well today, even if they lost.
      3. \n
    3. (G.B.) The Government are acting like themselves again.
      4. \n
    4. (G.B.) Have you got your grade in history yet?
      (U.S.) Have you gotten your grade in history yet?
      5. \n
    5. (G.B.) He went on a course. How many were on the course?
      (U.S.) He was in a course. How many were in the course?
      6. \n
    6. (G.B.) We lived in the High Street. (cf 'street people' ...)
      (U.S.) We lived on Main Street ("on" plus article plus High/Main)
      7. \n
    7. (G.B.) He's in hospital with a broken leg.
      (U.S.) He's in the hospital with a broken leg.
      8. \n
    8. (G.B.) I have got a car. vs. (U.S.) I have a car. I got a car. (different implications)
      9. \n
    9. (G.B.) We weren't able to catch him up
      (U.S.) We weren't able to catch him, catch up with him, catch up [with him].
      10. \n
    10. One was different from/than the other.
